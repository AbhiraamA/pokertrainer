Section 02

The Streets

A hand of poker is played across up to four betting rounds, called streets. New community cards are revealed on each street, changing the strength of every hand.

Preflop
🂠🂠
You get 2 hole cards. Blinds are posted. Betting starts left of the big blind.
Flop
🃟🃟🃟
3 community cards revealed. First postflop betting round.
Turn
🃟
4th community card revealed. Second postflop betting round.
River
🃟
5th and final card. Last betting round. Then showdown.
What are community cards?

Community cards (also called "the board") are dealt face-up in the middle of the table and are shared by all players. Everyone uses them to build their best hand.

You can use any combination of your 2 hole cards and the 5 community cards to make your best 5-card hand. That might mean using both hole cards, one, or neither (playing the board).

Blinds

Before any cards are dealt, the two players to the left of the dealer button post forced bets: the small blind (half a big blind) and the big blind. This creates a starting pot and gives players something to play for immediately. Everyone else acts relative to the big blind amount.

Showdown

If more than one player is still in after the final river betting round, everyone remaining flips their cards face-up. The player with the best 5-card hand wins the pot. If you fold before showdown, your cards are never revealed.

Section 03

Position

Position is where you sit relative to the dealer button. It's one of the most important concepts in poker — acting last gives you a huge advantage because you get to see what everyone else does before you decide.

UTG
Under the Gun — first to act preflop. You have zero information. Everyone else will act after you.
Worst
MP
Middle Position — slightly better. A few players have acted in front of you, but several remain behind.
Early
CO
Cutoff — one seat before the button. Late position; only the button acts after you postflop.
Good
BTN
Button (Dealer) — acts last on every postflop street. You see everyone else act before you decide. Maximum information.
Best
SB
Small Blind — posts half a big blind. Acts second-to-last preflop, but first on all postflop streets. Worst postflop position.
Weak
BB
Big Blind — posts a full big blind. Acts last preflop (advantage), but second-to-first postflop (disadvantage).
Mixed
💡

The golden rule: play fewer, stronger hands from early position (UTG, MP). Play more hands from late position (CO, BTN). The button is so powerful that hands unplayable from UTG become profitable from the button.

⚠️

The small blind looks cheap but it's the worst position postflop. You'll act first on every street after the flop — your opponents will always know your range of hands before they have to commit anything.

Section 04

Pot Odds

Pot odds tell you how often you need to win to make a call worthwhile. It's a simple comparison: what you're risking versus what you stand to gain.

Step-by-step example
Pot before the bet$100
Opponent bets$50
Total pot you'd win$100 + $50 = $150
You need to call$50
Pot odds calculation$50 ÷ ($100 + $50 + $50) = $50 ÷ $200 = 25%
Break-even thresholdYou need to win at least 25% of the time

If your equity (chance of winning the hand) is higher than 25%, calling is profitable in the long run. If it's lower, folding saves you money over time.

The ratio format: the pot is offering you 3:1 odds (win $150, risk $50). You need to win 1 out of every 4 times to break even — which is 25%.

Quick reference
Call $10 into a $10 pot$10 ÷ $30 = 33% needed (2:1 pot odds)
Call $25 into a $100 pot$25 ÷ $150 = 17% needed (5:1 pot odds)
Call $50 into a $50 pot$50 ÷ $150 = 33% needed (2:1 pot odds)
Call pot-sized betAlways 33% needed — remember this one
✓

This app shows pot odds as a ratio (e.g. 2:1) and a percentage (e.g. need 33% to call) in the action panel. Compare it to your equity — if equity > threshold, the call is profitable.

Outs

An out is any card remaining in the deck that would improve your hand enough to likely win. Counting your outs lets you estimate how often you'll improve — and whether calling is worthwhile.

Rule of 2 and 4

A fast way to estimate your equity from outs:

• On the flop (2 cards still to come): multiply outs × 4
• On the turn (1 card still to come): multiply outs × 2

Example: flush draw on the flop — 9 × 4 = 36%. This is an approximation; the app's Monte Carlo simulation gives you a more exact number.

⚠️

Not all outs are clean. If an 8 completes your straight but also gives your opponent a flush, it may not actually be a winning card. Experienced players discount "dirty" outs.

Section 06

Equity

Equity is your mathematical share of the pot if the hand were played out thousands of times. It doesn't mean you'll win this particular hand — it tells you what you'd win on average.

The coin flip analogy

Imagine flipping a coin for a $100 pot. You win half the time, so your equity is $50 — even before the flip happens. It doesn't matter that you'll either win $100 or $0 on any single flip; your expected value is always $50.

In poker: if you hold 70% equity in a $100 pot, your expected share is $70. If someone offers you the hand for $60, take it — it's worth more than you're paying. If they ask $75, fold — you'd be paying more than it's worth.

Equity changes on every street

Equity isn't fixed. Every new community card changes the calculation. You might have 70% equity on the flop, then spike to 95% when you hit your flush on the turn — or drop to 12% when an opponent completes their straight.

That's why experienced players don't just think about their current hand — they think about what cards could come and how those change the situation.

Two ways this app calculates equity
Outs-based (Rule of 2 & 4) — instant
Counts your outs and multiplies by 2 or 4 depending on the street. Returns in microseconds. Good rough estimate — shows instantly while you're deciding. Available on the flop and turn.
Monte Carlo simulation — more accurate ✓
The app deals out 400–600 complete random hands behind the scenes and counts how many you win. This catches scenarios outs-based math misses — opponent hands, blockers, board texture. Takes ~30ms and runs in the background. The coach always prefers this number.
✓

When both numbers are visible, trust Monte Carlo. It accounts for your specific opponents' likely holdings, not just raw card counts.

Section 07

What is GTO

GTO stands for Game Theory Optimal. It's a strategy that is mathematically unexploitable — even if your opponent knows exactly how you play, they cannot profit from that knowledge.

The rock-paper-scissors analogy

In rock-paper-scissors, the GTO strategy is to pick each option exactly ⅓ of the time at random. If you do this, your opponent cannot beat you — no matter what they throw, they'll win ⅓, lose ⅓, and tie ⅓ of the time.

But if you always throw rock, your opponent figures it out and always throws paper. You've become exploitable.

Poker works the same way. GTO means balancing how often you bet, check, call, and fold so opponents can't deduce your hand from your actions alone. You mix bluffs with value bets in mathematically correct proportions.

GTO vs Exploitative Play

GTO is a baseline — the floor below which you shouldn't drop. But against weak or predictable opponents, you can often do better by exploiting their specific mistakes. For example:

• A player who folds too often to bets → bluff them more than GTO says
• A player who calls every bet → never bluff, bet only your strong hands

The coach in this app evaluates your decisions against GTO principles. That doesn't mean GTO is always the highest-profit play — it means it's the hardest to punish.

💡

As a beginner, don't try to play perfect GTO. Focus on fundamentals first: fold weak hands, bet strong hands, call when pot odds justify it. GTO nuance comes later.

Section 08

Player Types

Most players fall into one of four archetypes based on two axes: how many hands they play (tight vs loose) and how aggressively they bet (aggressive vs passive). Recognizing the type tells you how to adjust.

TAG · Tight-Aggressive
Tight Aggressive
Plays few hands but bets and raises them hard. Usually strong when they enter a pot. The most common winning player type.
How to play against them: Respect their bets — they usually mean it. Don't bluff them into big pots. Wait for strong hands before stacking off.
LAG · Loose-Aggressive
Loose Aggressive / Maniac
Plays many hands and applies constant pressure with bets and raises. Often bluffing. Hard to read — could have anything.
How to play against them: Let them bluff into you. Call down with medium-strength hands you'd fold against a TAG. Don't re-bluff a maniac.
Rock · Tight-Passive
Tight Passive (Rock)
Plays few hands and rarely bets or raises even when they're strong. They call a lot and check when they should bet.
How to play against them: When a rock raises, fold unless you have a very strong hand. Steal their blinds frequently — they won't fight back.
STA · Calling Station
Loose Passive (Calling Station)
Plays many hands and calls almost every bet but rarely raises. Will call with any pair, any draw, any gutshot.
How to play against them: Never bluff a calling station — they'll call. Instead, bet your strong hands for maximum value. They'll pay you off.
💡

The bots in this app are one of four types: TAG, LAG, Maniac, and Calling Station (STA). Each has a label shown on the felt and in the player list.

Section 09

Common Mistakes Beginners Make

Almost every beginner leaks money in the same predictable ways. Recognizing these mistakes in your own game is worth more than any advanced strategy.

1
Playing too many hands preflop
Every hand you voluntarily enter costs money. Folding 7-2 offsuit before the flop costs you nothing. Calling a raise with it and then calling three more streets costs you everything. Tighten your preflop range — especially from early position.
2
Ignoring position
Playing a mediocre hand from UTG is a losing play. Playing that same hand from the button can be fine. Many beginners play the same range regardless of where they sit. Acting last is worth a lot — account for it.
3
Ignoring pot odds
Calling a $50 bet into a $50 pot because you "might hit" isn't enough. You need to win 33% of the time to break even there. If your equity is 20%, you're losing money on every call — even the ones you eventually win.
4
Chasing draws without the odds
A gutshot draw (4 outs) has about 8% equity on the turn. Calling a pot-sized bet to hit it requires 33% equity. The math doesn't work — but beginners call anyway because "I had a draw." Only chase when the pot odds justify it.
5
Not betting strong hands for value
Slowplaying (checking a monster hand to trap) sounds clever but often backfires. You give opponents free cards to outdraw you and miss value from hands that would have called a bet. When you're strong, usually bet.
6
Folding too easily to aggression
When an opponent bets, it doesn't mean they have a strong hand. If you have top pair and fold every time someone bets, aggressive players will exploit you constantly. Compare your equity to the pot odds before folding.
7
Treating every hand identically regardless of stack size
With 10 big blinds, you should often go all-in preflop with any decent hand — you don't have room to play postflop. With 200 big blinds, you can fold hands that would be mandatory shoves with a short stack. Stack size changes the math significantly.

Glossary

Every term used in this app, defined in plain English.

All-inBetting all your remaining chips. Once all-in, you can't win more than the amount you put in from any single player (side pots apply if multiple players are all-in for different amounts).
BB (Big Blind)The forced bet posted by the player two seats left of the dealer. Sets the minimum bet size for the preflop round.
BluffBetting or raising with a weak hand to make opponents fold stronger hands. A bluff wins by making everyone else fold — not by showdown.
BTN (Button)The dealer position, marked by the dealer button. Acts last on every postflop street — the most powerful position at the table.
CallMatching the current bet to stay in the hand without raising. You don't add more — you match what's been bet.
CheckDeclining to bet when no bet has been made yet in this round. Keeps you in the hand for free. Not available if someone has already bet.
Community cardsThe shared cards dealt face-up in the middle of the table (flop, turn, river). Every player uses them to build their best hand.
DrawAn incomplete hand that needs one or more cards to become strong. Examples: flush draw, straight draw, gutshot. You're "on a draw" when you're betting on improving.
EquityYour mathematical share of the pot if the hand were played out many times. 60% equity in a $100 pot = you'd win $60 on average.
FlopThe first three community cards, revealed simultaneously. Triggers the first postflop betting round.
FoldDiscarding your hand and giving up your claim to the pot. You lose any chips already invested but risk nothing more.
GTOGame Theory Optimal. A mathematically balanced strategy that cannot be exploited — even a player who knows your strategy can't profit from it. Used as a benchmark for decision quality.
Hand rangeThe set of all possible hands a player might hold given their actions. Instead of guessing one exact hand, you think in terms of ranges: "they could have any pair plus strong aces."
Hole cardsThe two private cards dealt face-down to each player at the start of a hand. Only you can see them (until showdown).
Implied oddsThe additional chips you expect to win on future streets if you hit your hand. A draw that doesn't justify a call on pure pot odds can still be worth playing if you'll win a huge pot when you hit.
LAGLoose-Aggressive. A player type that plays many hands and bets/raises aggressively. Hard to read; frequently bluffs.
Made handA complete hand that doesn't need improvement to be strong — e.g. a flopped flush, a set, top pair. Opposite of a draw.
MAN (Maniac)An extreme loose-aggressive player who raises and re-raises almost every hand. Profitable to let them bluff into strong hands.
Monte Carlo simulationA method of calculating equity by simulating thousands of random runouts. More accurate than outs-based estimates because it accounts for actual card distribution and opponent hands.
OvercardA hole card that ranks higher than any card on the board. If the board is 8-5-3 and you hold an Ace, that Ace is an overcard — it might pair on the next street.
OutsSpecific cards remaining in the deck that would improve your hand enough to likely win. Flush draw = 9 outs; open-ended straight draw = 8 outs; gutshot = 4 outs.
PositionWhere you sit relative to the dealer button. Later position = more information = better position. The button is the best; the small blind is the worst postflop.
PotThe total chips at stake in the current hand, contributed by all bets and blinds. The winner takes the pot.
Pot oddsThe ratio of the amount you need to call compared to the total pot (after you call). Tells you the minimum equity you need to make the call mathematically profitable.
PreflopThe first betting round, before any community cards are dealt. Players act based only on their two hole cards.
RaiseIncreasing the size of the current bet. Forces other players to call, re-raise, or fold. Also called a "bet" when you're the first to put chips in (no prior bet to raise).
RiverThe fifth and final community card. After the river betting round, any remaining players go to showdown.
SB (Small Blind)The forced bet posted by the player directly left of the dealer button. Half the size of the big blind. Worst postflop position.
ShowdownThe reveal of all remaining players' hole cards after the river betting is complete. Best 5-card hand wins the pot.
StackThe total chips a player currently has at the table. Your stack limits how much you can bet or lose in a hand.
STA (Calling Station)A loose-passive player who calls almost every bet but rarely raises. Beat them by betting strong hands — they'll pay you off. Never bluff them.
TAGTight-Aggressive. Plays a narrow range of strong hands and bets them forcefully. The most common winning player style.
To callThe amount of chips you need to put in to match the current bet and stay in the hand. Shown in the app's action panel.
TurnThe fourth community card, dealt after the flop betting round. Triggers the third betting round.
UTG (Under the Gun)The player immediately left of the big blind. Acts first preflop — the worst position because you have no information about how anyone else will act.
Value betBetting with a strong hand specifically to get called by weaker hands. The goal is to extract chips from opponents who have second-best hands.
VerdictThe coach's assessment of your decision: Optimal (best possible play), Acceptable (reasonable, not perfect), or Mistake (a play that costs you expected value long-term).
